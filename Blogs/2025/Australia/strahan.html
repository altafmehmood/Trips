Our day began in Strahan with a once-in-a-lifetime experience cruising through one of the world's last great wilderness areas, followed by our journey to Tasmania's most iconic mountain landscape.

Gordon River Cruise - Into the Wilderness

The Gordon River Cruise is one of Tasmania's most spectacular experiences. Departing from Strahan's waterfront, the cruise takes you across Macquarie Harbour - one of Australia's largest natural harbors, six times the size of Sydney Harbour - and deep into the heart of the Tasmanian Wilderness World Heritage Area.

As we crossed Macquarie Harbour toward the open ocean, we passed Bonnet Island with its distinctive lighthouse. Built in 1889, the Bonnet Island Lighthouse marks the treacherous entrance to Macquarie Harbour known as Hells Gates - a narrow 120-meter wide channel where the Southern Ocean surges through with tremendous force. The lighthouse has guided countless ships safely through these perilous waters, warning mariners of the dangerous rocks and fierce currents that have claimed many vessels over the centuries. Standing sentinel on this remote island, the lighthouse represents both the danger and the vital navigation aids that made safe passage possible in this wild corner of Tasmania.

The journey up the Gordon River is otherworldly. The river flows dark and still, stained a tea-brown color by tannins from the surrounding buttongrass plains and ancient forests. This pristine water is so pure and acidic that it preserves fallen Huon pine logs for thousands of years on the river bottom. The forest-clad hills rise steeply from the water's edge, creating a sense of complete wilderness isolation.

Sarah Island - Harsh Convict History

The cruise includes a stop at Sarah Island, a former penal settlement that was once considered the harshest convict station in the British Empire. Between 1822 and 1833, convicts endured brutal conditions here, logging Huon pine in freezing conditions while chained together. Many attempted escape, though few succeeded in crossing the treacherous Southern Ocean beyond.

Walking through the sparse ruins, you can feel the weight of history. The island's isolation in the middle of massive Macquarie Harbour made it the perfect prison - escape was nearly impossible. The convicts built ships here from the prized Huon pine, ironically crafting the very vessels that would eventually transport them to other destinations. The island's history is haunting, yet the natural beauty surrounding it is undeniable.

Heritage Landing - Ancient Rainforest

The highlight of the cruise is Heritage Landing, where we disembarked to walk through one of the last remaining stands of ancient Huon pines and massive myrtle beech trees. This 2,000-year-old rainforest is utterly primeval - trees that were already ancient when the Romans walked the Earth still stand here, their trunks draped in moss and lichen.

The boardwalk winds through the forest, protecting the fragile ecosystem while allowing close encounters with these magnificent trees. Huon pines are endemic to Tasmania and among the oldest living organisms on Earth - they grow incredibly slowly, with some trees over 3,000 years old. The timber is so dense and resinous that fallen logs can remain intact on the riverbed for millennia.

The silence of this rainforest, broken only by bird calls and the gentle sound of water, is almost spiritual. Standing among these ancient giants gives you a profound sense of the timescales of nature and the importance of preserving these last wilderness areas.

Drive to Cradle Mountain

After the cruise, we drove from Strahan to Cradle Mountain-Lake St Clair National Park. The drive takes you through changing landscapes - from the barren, moonscape-like hills around Queenstown (stripped bare by 19th-century mining operations) to the pristine wilderness of the national park.

Evening Wildlife Spotting - Wombats & Marsupials

Arriving at Cradle Mountain in the late afternoon, we first checked into Cradle Mountain Lodge, a beautiful wilderness retreat nestled at the edge of the national park. The lodge offers comfortable accommodation with stunning mountain views, perfectly positioned for exploring the park.

After settling in, we drove to Ronny Creek for an evening wildlife walk. One of the advantages of visiting after 6:30pm is that you can drive your own vehicle all the way into the park without needing to take the shuttle bus - the park opens up private vehicle access outside peak hours. This made it easy to reach the best wildlife viewing spots in the evening light.

The late afternoon and early evening hours are prime time for spotting Tasmania's unique marsupials, and we weren't disappointed. Wombats were everywhere - these stocky, powerful diggers were grazing peacefully in the button grass plains, completely unfazed by our presence. We watched them waddle about on their surprisingly short legs, munching grass and occasionally marking their territory. Wombats can weigh up to 35 kilograms and are incredibly strong, using their powerful claws to dig extensive burrow systems.

We also spotted several Bennett's wallabies bounding across the moorland with their distinctive hopping gait, and heard the calls of countless bird species including the beautiful pink robin and the endemic Tasmanian native-hen. The alpine landscape at dusk, with wildlife moving freely through the pristine wilderness, felt utterly magical.

As the sun began to set, Cradle Mountain's iconic jagged peak glowed in the golden light, promising spectacular hiking for the following day.
