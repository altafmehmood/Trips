Our journey from Hobart to Strahan took us through some of Tasmania's most spectacular national parks and wilderness areas. This epic drive west across the island showcases the diversity of Tasmania's landscapes - from mountain rainforests and glacial lakes to ancient temperate rainforest and wild rivers.

Mount Field National Park - Russell Falls & Tall Trees

Our first stop was Mount Field National Park, Tasmania's oldest national park established in 1916. The park protects an extraordinary range of habitats from eucalyptus forests at lower elevations to alpine moorland on the plateau, with some of the most accessible and beautiful waterfalls in Tasmania.

The Russell Falls Track is an easy 20-minute walk through enchanting temperate rainforest to the park's most famous feature. Russell Falls cascades in three tiers over moss-covered rocks, surrounded by massive tree ferns that create a prehistoric atmosphere. The falls are particularly impressive after rain, though we were fortunate to see them flowing well even in summer.

The rainforest here is magnificent - dominated by massive swamp gums (the world's tallest flowering plant), myrtle beech draped in moss and lichen, and towering tree ferns creating a lush canopy overhead. The well-maintained boardwalk allows you to experience this ancient forest without impacting the delicate ecosystem.

We continued on the Tall Trees Walk, a circuit track through some of the most impressive old-growth forest in Tasmania. These enormous swamp gums tower over 80 meters high, their straight trunks soaring up to a distant canopy. Walking among these giants - some hundreds of years old - gives you a profound sense of the timescales of nature.

Journey Through the Central Highlands

The drive from Mount Field to Lake St Clair takes you through the beautiful Central Highlands, a region of rolling hills, pastoral farmland, and remnant forests. The small town of Ouse marks the midpoint of this scenic journey, where the landscape transitions from the lowland forests to the glacial highlands beyond.

Lake St Clair - Australia's Deepest Lake

Continuing west, we arrived at Lake St Clair, the deepest freshwater lake in Australia at over 200 meters deep. The lake was carved by glaciers during the ice ages and sits at the southern end of the famous Overland Track, one of Australia's premier multi-day hiking routes.

The Cynthia Bay Foreshore Walk is a gentle track along the lake's edge, offering stunning views across the pristine glacial waters to the mountains beyond. The water is incredibly clear but freezing cold - fed by mountain streams and retaining its glacial chill year-round. On calm days, the lake's surface perfectly mirrors the surrounding peaks and ancient forests.

The area has a profound sense of wilderness and solitude. The silence is broken only by the calls of black currawongs and the gentle lapping of water on the rocky shore. It's easy to understand why this area is part of the Tasmanian Wilderness World Heritage Area - it feels utterly untouched by human development.

Franklin-Gordon Wild Rivers National Park

Franklin-Gordon Wild Rivers National Park protects some of the last untamed rivers in the world and was the site of one of Australia's most significant environmental campaigns in the 1980s. The successful campaign to prevent the damming of the Franklin River helped establish Tasmania's wilderness areas as World Heritage listed.

The Nelson Falls walk is a short 20-minute loop through primeval rainforest to a beautiful waterfall. The forest here is even more ancient and untouched than Mount Field - massive myrtle beech trees hundreds of years old, their trunks cloaked in thick moss and epiphytic ferns. The falls themselves drop in a silvery curtain through the green gloom of the rainforest.

At Donaghys Hill Lookout, we stopped for sweeping views across the wild rivers region. From this elevated viewpoint, the landscape stretches to the horizon - endless forested mountains, deep valleys carved by ancient rivers, and not a single sign of human presence. It's a landscape that has remained essentially unchanged for millions of years.

The Franklin River Nature Trail descends to the edge of the wild Franklin River itself. This relatively short walk (30 minutes return) takes you down through Huon pine forest to the river's edge. Standing beside these dark, tannin-stained waters, you're touching a piece of living history - the Franklin River has been flowing through these ancient forests since before humans walked the earth.

The Huon pines along the trail are particularly special - this endemic Tasmanian tree is one of the longest-living organisms on earth, with some trees over 2,000 years old. They grow incredibly slowly in these wet, cool conditions, their timber so dense and resinous that fallen logs can remain intact for thousands of years.

Evening in Queenstown and Strahan

After our wilderness explorations, we stopped in the historic mining town of Queenstown for dinner at Mimso Kitchen, a local favorite serving fresh Tasmanian cuisine with Asian influences. The seafood here is exceptional - caught fresh from the surrounding waters and prepared with creative flair.

We then continued our drive to Strahan, nestled on the shores of massive Macquarie Harbour on Tasmania's wild west coast. This charming fishing village is the gateway to the Gordon River and Sarah Island's convict history.

We checked into Strahan Village, perfectly located on the waterfront. The village-style accommodation offers comfortable rooms with views over the harbor, and the property includes excellent dining options and a cozy atmosphere that perfectly complements Strahan's laid-back charm.

West Coast Wilderness: The drive from Hobart to Strahan takes you through the heart of Tasmania's World Heritage wilderness. Allow a full day and stop at Mount Field, Lake St Clair, and Franklin-Gordon Wild Rivers National Park to truly appreciate the journey.
