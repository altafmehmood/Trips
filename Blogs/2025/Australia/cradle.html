The morning light was perfect for photography, and we had the trails largely to ourselves.

Dove Lake Circuit - Tasmania's Most Iconic Walk

The Dove Lake Circuit is Tasmania's most famous walk, and for good reason. The iconic jagged peak of Cradle Mountain, rising 1,545 meters, reflected perfectly in Dove Lake's mirror-like surface in the calm morning conditions, creating one of those postcard-perfect scenes that no camera can truly capture. The distinctive serrated profile of the mountain dominates the landscape, formed by millions of years of glacial action that carved these dramatic dolerite peaks.

This 6-kilometer loop track showcases the best of Cradle Mountain's alpine environment. The well-maintained trail takes 2-3 hours and can be walked in either direction. We chose to walk clockwise, saving the most dramatic mountain views for the second half of the circuit.

The walk took us through remarkably diverse terrain - from beach pebbles along the shoreline where we could touch the crystal-clear glacial waters, across sturdy boardwalks through button grass plains that glow golden in the right light, and finally through ancient myrtle beech forests draped in moss and lichen. Each section offered a completely different perspective on this extraordinary landscape.

The weather at Cradle Mountain is famously changeable - it's said you can experience four seasons in one day. We were fortunate with mostly clear skies in the morning, perfect conditions for this spectacular walk.

Cradle Lake Lookout - Hidden Alpine Gem

After completing the Dove Lake Circuit, we drove to Ronny Creek parking lot to tackle another spectacular walk. The trail to Cradle Lake Lookout follows part of the famous Overland Track, Tasmania's premier multi-day hiking route that traverses 65 kilometers through the heart of the Tasmanian Wilderness World Heritage Area.

This shorter walk offers a taste of the Overland Track's alpine beauty without the multi-day commitment. The trail climbs steadily through button grass moorland and ancient forest, offering increasingly spectacular views back towards Cradle Mountain. The lookout provides stunning vistas over Cradle Lake, a pristine glacial tarn nestled in the mountain valley below.

The morning's exertion was rewarded with the peaceful solitude of the alpine environment and the sense of being deep in wilderness, surrounded by mountains that have stood unchanged for millennia.

Devils@Cradle - Tasmanian Devil Conservation

After our morning hikes, we visited Devils@Cradle, a wildlife sanctuary dedicated to conserving the endangered Tasmanian devil. This conservation park plays a crucial role in protecting Tasmania's iconic marsupial carnivore from the devastating facial tumor disease that has decimated wild populations.

We arrived in time for the feeding session, where we watched both Tasmanian devils and spotted-tail quolls being fed. Seeing these remarkable animals up close was unforgettable - devils are much larger and more powerful than many people expect, with incredibly strong jaws capable of crushing bones. Their fierce reputation is well-earned, though the sanctuary keepers explained their complex social behaviors and the conservation challenges they face.

Spotted-tail quolls, Australia's largest carnivorous marsupial after the Tasmanian devil, were equally impressive. These beautiful spotted animals are agile climbers and fierce predators despite their cat-like appearance. Watching them feed demonstrated their powerful hunting abilities.

The visit provided not just an opportunity to see these endangered species, but also to learn about the critical conservation work being done to ensure their survival for future generations.

Drive to Freycinet Peninsula

After our morning of hiking and wildlife encounters, we faced a challenging 4-hour drive from Cradle Mountain to Freycinet Peninsula on Tasmania's east coast. This cross-country journey takes you through the heart of Tasmania, from the rugged west coast wilderness to the pristine beaches of the Tasman Sea.

The route is spectacular but demanding - winding mountain roads with countless hairpin turns, steep descents, and dramatic elevation changes. The road snakes through dense forests, past highland lakes, and over mountain passes, requiring constant attention and careful driving. While challenging, the ever-changing scenery makes every turn worthwhile.

As we descended toward the east coast, the landscape transformed from alpine wilderness to coastal forests and finally to the distinctive pink granite formations of Freycinet. The late afternoon light was perfect as we arrived at our destination.

We checked into Freycinet Lodge, nestled in the heart of Freycinet National Park. The eco-friendly lodge offers comfortable accommodation with stunning views of the surrounding bush and Great Oyster Bay. After the long drive, arriving at this peaceful coastal retreat felt like entering paradise.

The evening light over Coles Bay was magical, with the pink granite Hazards mountains glowing in the setting sun. We explored the lodge grounds and walked down to the nearby beach, enjoying the serenity after our adventurous day.
