Day 5: Grey Mare's Tail & Glasgow
January 14 ‚Ä¢ Waterfall Hike & Journey to Scotland's Largest City

Our fifth day began with a farewell to the Lake District as we set out northward toward Glasgow. Rather than rushing straight to the city, we planned a significant stop at one of Scotland's most spectacular natural attractions: the Grey Mare's Tail waterfall in Dumfries and Galloway.

The drive from Grasmere took us through the Scottish Borders, where the landscape gradually transformed from the English Lake District's characteristic fells to Scotland's distinctive rolling hills and dramatic valleys. Dumfries and Galloway, often overlooked by travelers rushing between major cities, proved to be a region of remarkable natural beauty - exactly why we'd chosen to break our journey here for a proper hike.

Grey Mare's Tail Waterfall Hike

The Grey Mare's Tail is one of Scotland's highest waterfalls, a 60-meter (200-foot) cascade that plunges down a hanging valley formed during the last ice age. Managed by the National Trust for Scotland, the site offers a dramatic hike that combines stunning waterfall views with the option to climb to Loch Skeen, a remote mountain loch sitting in a glacial corrie above the falls.

We arrived at the National Trust car park and immediately felt the shift to genuine Scottish Highlands terrain. The valley here is steep-sided and wild, with the Tail Burn rushing down through a gorge before plunging over the cliff edge as the Grey Mare's Tail. Even from the parking area, the scale of the landscape was impressive - this was bigger, wilder terrain than the Lake District.

The hike begins with a steep climb alongside the waterfall. The path is rough, rocky, and requires good fitness and proper hiking boots - especially in January when conditions can be wet and icy. As we climbed, the views of the waterfall became increasingly spectacular. The white water cascading down the cliff face created a mesmerizing display, especially dramatic given the winter flow swollen by recent rainfall.

Continuing beyond the waterfall, the trail climbs into increasingly remote terrain. The landscape opened up into a high mountain valley - vast, treeless, and utterly wild. Snow clung to the higher slopes, and the wind carried the unmistakable bite of the Scottish Highlands. This was serious mountain country, and the weather reminded us that January hiking in Scotland demands respect.

After a sustained climb, we reached Loch Skeen, sitting at around 510 meters elevation in its glacial bowl. The loch was partially frozen, with intricate ice patterns along the shoreline and snow-capped peaks rising on all sides. The scene was stark and beautiful - a classic Scottish mountain loch in winter conditions, accessible only to those willing to make the steep climb from the valley below.

The surrounding peaks, though not towering, created a genuine sense of remoteness. We had the loch almost entirely to ourselves - unsurprising given the January date and challenging conditions. The silence was profound, broken only by the wind and the distant sound of the waterfall far below. This was the Scotland of wilderness and solitude, far removed from the tourist trails.

Grey Mare's Tail Hiking Guide: The hike to Loch Skeen is approximately 5-6 miles round trip with significant elevation gain (~500m). The path is steep, rocky, and can be treacherous when wet or icy - proper hiking boots essential. Allow 3-4 hours for the full hike. Winter conditions add serious challenges - check weather forecasts and be prepared for wind, rain, or snow. The National Trust car park has limited facilities. This is genuine mountain terrain requiring appropriate preparation and fitness. Day's Activity: 14,827 steps covering 7.0 miles - matching our most active Lake District day!

Drive to Glasgow

After completing the Grey Mare's Tail hike, we continued our journey northwest toward Glasgow. The drive took us through more of Dumfries and Galloway's beautiful countryside before approaching Scotland's largest city. After hours of remote mountain terrain, Glasgow's urban energy came as a striking contrast.

We checked in at the voco Grand Central Glasgow, a beautifully restored Victorian hotel located directly above Glasgow Central Station. The hotel's location couldn't be more convenient - right in the heart of the city with easy access to shopping, dining, and attractions. Our room overlooked the bustling streets of central Glasgow, a dramatic shift from the wilderness solitude of Loch Skeen just hours earlier.

After the active morning hiking in the mountains, we had the afternoon to relax at the hotel before venturing out for dinner. The contrast between the wild remoteness of Loch Skeen in the morning and the comfortable urban environment of central Glasgow provided a welcome change of pace.

Exploring Glasgow

Glasgow is Scotland's largest city and often plays second fiddle to Edinburgh in tourist itineraries - but that's part of its charm. Where Edinburgh is polished and historic, Glasgow is gritty and authentic, with a vibrant arts scene, striking Victorian and Art Nouveau architecture, and a welcoming, down-to-earth atmosphere that Glaswegians are rightfully proud of.

The area around our hotel offered a perfect introduction to Glasgow's character. Buchanan Street, one of the city's main shopping thoroughfares, is part of the "Style Mile" - a network of pedestrianized streets lined with shops, caf√©s, and stunning Victorian buildings. The grand facades, many designed during Glasgow's wealthy shipbuilding era, speak to the city's prosperous past as the "Second City of the Empire."

Glasgow is also renowned for the work of Charles Rennie Mackintosh, the visionary architect and designer whose Art Nouveau masterpieces dot the city. Though we didn't have time for extended sightseeing, his influence is visible everywhere - from the distinctive lettering styles to the Glasgow School of Art (sadly damaged by fire but undergoing restoration). The city's architectural heritage creates a fascinating urban landscape that rewards wandering.

The voco Grand Central's location above Glasgow Central Station placed us at the crossroads of the city's transport network, with the magnificent Victorian station hall below us. The station itself, opened in 1879, is a destination in its own right with its ornate ironwork, grand staircase, and constant buzz of travelers. Standing at the heart of Glasgow's railway heritage felt appropriate for a day spent exploring Scotland's varied landscapes by road.

Dinner at Dishoom Glasgow

To conclude our first day in Glasgow, we returned to familiar culinary territory: Dishoom Glasgow - our second Dishoom of the trip after Manchester on Day 2.

The Glasgow location is particularly special, occupying a stunning space in a former bank building on Buchanan Street. The grand Victorian interior - ornate ceilings, original columns, banking hall proportions - provides a unique backdrop for Dishoom's signature Bombay caf√© aesthetic. The combination of Glasgow's historic architecture and the restaurant's retro-Bombay design creates something you won't find at the other locations.

After a morning scrambling up to Loch Skeen, the familiar comfort of Dishoom's Black Daal, fresh naan, and warming chai was exactly what we needed. The consistency across their locations is remarkable - the same quality, same welcoming atmosphere, same satisfying flavors we'd enjoyed in Manchester now in a completely different architectural context.

As we walked back to our hotel through Glasgow's illuminated streets, we reflected on the contrasts of Day 5 - from the wild remoteness of Loch Skeen in the morning to the urban sophistication of central Glasgow in the evening, from steep mountain trails to comfortable restaurant dining. This range of experiences captured what makes Scotland such a compelling destination.
