Day 6: Edinburgh
January 15 ‚Ä¢ Glasgow to Edinburgh & City Arrival

After our explorations of Glasgow, we departed for Edinburgh - Scotland's historic capital. The drive from Glasgow to Edinburgh takes just over an hour along the M8 motorway, connecting Scotland's two great cities. As we approached Edinburgh, the skyline transformed dramatically: the volcanic crags of Arthur's Seat and Calton Hill rose above the Georgian and medieval architecture, creating one of Europe's most distinctive urban landscapes.

We checked into the W Edinburgh, a luxury hotel occupying a prime location on St Andrew Square in the heart of Edinburgh's New Town. The hotel's contemporary design stands in striking contrast to the Georgian architecture surrounding it, offering modern luxury with panoramic city views.

New Town vs Old Town: Edinburgh is famously divided into two distinct sections. The Old Town, centered on the Royal Mile, dates to medieval times with narrow wynds (alleys) and towering tenements. The New Town, built in the 18th century, showcases Georgian elegance with wide streets, crescents, and symmetrical squares. Together, they form a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Breakfast at Society Bar and Kitchen

While waiting for our room to be ready, we crossed the road to Society Bar and Kitchen, a stylish restaurant and bar located just steps from the W Edinburgh. The restaurant offered the perfect spot to refuel after the drive from Glasgow and plan our first day in Edinburgh.

Society serves modern Scottish cuisine with a contemporary twist in a warm, welcoming atmosphere. We enjoyed a leisurely breakfast, taking advantage of the convenient location and quality food. The restaurant's proximity to the hotel made it an easy choice, and the meal provided the perfect start to our Edinburgh exploration.

Calton Hill Walk

After breakfast, we set out for Calton Hill, one of Edinburgh's iconic viewpoints located just a short walk from our hotel. This volcanic hill rises 103 meters above the city and is crowned with several distinctive monuments that form part of Edinburgh's UNESCO World Heritage Site. The walk up from Regent Road takes just 10-15 minutes and offers increasingly spectacular views with every step.

Calton Hill is famous for its collection of neoclassical monuments, earning Edinburgh its nickname "Athens of the North." The most prominent is the National Monument - an unfinished replica of the Parthenon that sits dramatically on the summit. Also on the hill are the Nelson Monument (a tower commemorating Admiral Nelson's victory at Trafalgar), the City Observatory, and several other classical structures. The combination of ancient Greek-inspired architecture against Edinburgh's medieval and Georgian backdrop creates a uniquely striking scene.

The views from Calton Hill are spectacular and comprehensive. Looking west, Edinburgh Castle commands its rocky perch above the Old Town. The New Town's Georgian streets spread out to the north with the Firth of Forth visible beyond. Arthur's Seat rises to the east, its volcanic profile unmistakable. To the south, the Royal Mile cuts through the Old Town, with countless spires and historic buildings creating Edinburgh's famous skyline. It's one of the best spots for photography in the entire city, especially in the soft winter light we experienced.

We spent considerable time on Calton Hill, exploring the monuments and taking in the 360-degree panoramas. The site was relatively quiet on this January afternoon - a stark contrast to the summer months when it becomes crowded with tourists and locals alike. The winter visit allowed us to appreciate the space at our own pace and truly absorb Edinburgh's remarkable setting.

Dinner at Sushisamba

To conclude our first day in Edinburgh, we had reservations at Sushisamba, located on the 10th floor of the W Edinburgh. This upscale restaurant offers a unique fusion of Japanese, Brazilian, and Peruvian cuisine - a culinary combination that sounds unexpected but works brilliantly in execution.

The restaurant's location provides stunning panoramic views across Edinburgh. Floor-to-ceiling windows frame the illuminated cityscape: Edinburgh Castle glowing on its rock, the spires and rooftops of the Old Town, the lights of Princes Street, and Calton Hill's monuments silhouetted against the evening sky. Dining with these views transformed the meal into a complete Edinburgh experience.

The menu showcased Sushisamba's signature fusion approach. Fresh sushi and sashimi reflected Japanese precision, while Brazilian churrasco influences and Peruvian anticuchos brought bold flavors and grilled elements. The presentation was artful, the flavors balanced, and the service attentive. It was the perfect celebratory dinner for our arrival in Scotland's capital, combining international cuisine with uniquely Edinburgh views.

As we finished dinner and looked out over the illuminated city, we reflected on how perfectly this first day had captured Edinburgh's character - historic monuments and neoclassical architecture, stunning viewpoints and volcanic landscapes, and contemporary luxury dining with traditional city views. Edinburgh was already living up to its reputation as one of Europe's most beautiful capitals.

Arthur's Seat Summit Hike

After breakfast, we walked from the W Edinburgh through the city to reach the start of the Arthur's Seat hike. The walk took us through Edinburgh's streets and into Holyrood Park, the remarkable 640-acre royal park that sits right in the heart of the city. Arthur's Seat, the 251-meter (823-foot) peak that dominates Edinburgh's skyline, is an extinct volcano - a piece of dramatic wilderness within an urban landscape.

We began our ascent around 10:30 AM, following the main trail from The Dry Dam area. The path immediately starts climbing, winding upward through volcanic terrain that reveals Edinburgh's ancient geological history. The January morning provided perfect hiking conditions - cool temperatures, clear skies, and excellent visibility.

The climb is moderately challenging, gaining significant elevation over a relatively short distance. The trail alternates between well-maintained paths and rockier sections requiring careful footing. The volcanic rock formations - basalt columns, ancient lava flows, and exposed geological strata - create dramatic scenery and fascinating geology visible throughout the climb. We passed other early morning hikers, all drawn by the promise of spectacular views from the summit.

Reaching the summit of Arthur's Seat around 10:56 AM rewarded us with breathtaking 360-degree panoramic views across Edinburgh and far beyond. To the north, the Firth of Forth gleamed in the morning light with the bridges spanning to Fife visible in the distance. The entire city spread below us: the medieval Old Town clustered around the Royal Mile, the Georgian New Town with its orderly streets and crescents, Edinburgh Castle commanding its volcanic rock, and Calton Hill with its neoclassical monuments where we'd stood the previous day.

The views extended for miles in every direction. We could see the Pentland Hills to the south, the Ochil Hills rising to the west, and on this clear morning, even distant Highland mountains on the horizon. The scale and beauty of the landscape was extraordinary - Edinburgh's perfect blend of natural drama and architectural heritage fully revealed from this elevated perspective.

We spent considerable time at the summit, taking in the views from all angles and capturing photos. The sense of achievement from reaching the peak, combined with the spectacular scenery, made this one of the highlights of our entire UK trip. The fact that such a dramatic natural feature exists within city limits makes Arthur's Seat truly unique among European capitals.

Arthur's Seat Hiking Guide: The extinct volcano offers several routes to the summit. The main path from the Holyrood Park area is the most popular (moderate difficulty, 1.5-2 hours round trip including summit time). Wear proper hiking shoes with good grip - the volcanic rock can be slippery. Early morning provides the best light for photography and fewer crowds. The 360-degree views from the summit make it Edinburgh's best viewpoint. Start early to beat the crowds and enjoy the best conditions. Day's Activity: 20,688 steps covering 9.8 miles - Arthur's Seat summit hike plus extensive Old Town exploration made this our second most active day!

Afternoon Exploration: University & Royal Mile

After lunch and a brief rest at the hotel, we set out for an afternoon walk through Edinburgh's academic and historic heart. We made our way to the University of Edinburgh campus, one of Scotland's ancient universities founded in 1582. The university buildings spread throughout the city center, blending seamlessly with Edinburgh's architectural heritage. Walking through the campus areas gave us a sense of Edinburgh's long intellectual tradition and its continuing role as a major academic center.

From the university area, we walked to the Royal Mile - the historic street that runs through the heart of the Old Town from Edinburgh Castle down to the Palace of Holyroodhouse. This iconic thoroughfare, technically comprised of several connecting streets (Castlehill, Lawnmarket, High Street, and Canongate), has been Edinburgh's main artery for centuries.

Walking the Royal Mile in the afternoon, we experienced Edinburgh's unique medieval atmosphere. The street is lined with historic buildings, many dating back hundreds of years. The medieval closes (narrow passageways) branching off the main street led into hidden courtyards and staircases - atmospheric alleyways that provided glimpses into Edinburgh's layered past. Historic landmarks lined our route: St Giles' Cathedral with its distinctive crown spire, John Knox House, and countless shops, pubs, and restaurants occupying buildings that have served the city for generations.

Edinburgh Castle loomed at the western end of the Royal Mile, perched on its volcanic rock 130 meters above sea level. While we didn't tour the castle interior, its commanding presence defines Edinburgh's skyline and history. The fortress has been a royal residence, military garrison, and prison over its thousand-year history, and remains one of Scotland's most iconic landmarks.

Our two days in Edinburgh provided an incredible glimpse of Scotland's capital. We experienced its dramatic landscape climbing Arthur's Seat, enjoyed spectacular views from both the summit and the W Edinburgh, explored its medieval Old Town along the Royal Mile, walked through its historic university, and discovered its excellent culinary scene. The city's blend of natural drama (extinct volcanoes within city limits), architectural heritage (medieval and Georgian UNESCO sites), and modern sophistication (luxury hotels, fine dining, contemporary culture) creates an unforgettable destination worthy of much longer exploration.

Ancient Geology: Edinburgh's dramatic topography results from volcanic activity 350 million years ago during the Carboniferous period. Arthur's Seat is the remains of an extinct volcano, while Edinburgh Castle sits on a volcanic plug - the solidified magma that once filled a volcano's vent. During the Ice Age, glaciers carved around these hard volcanic rocks, creating the distinctive crag-and-tail formations visible throughout the city. This geology creates Edinburgh's unique character - a city built on and around ancient volcanoes.
