Edinburgh to London Drive

Day 8 began with an early morning departure from Edinburgh at 8:00 AM. After a week exploring Scotland and northern England, it was time to return to London for our final evening and flight home. We packed up our belongings at the W Edinburgh, loaded our SIXT BMW 320 Station Wagon, and set out for the long drive south.

Our route took us from Edinburgh through Carlisle on the Scottish-English border, then southwest through Blackpool on England's northwest coast before turning south and bypassing Birmingham on our way to Greater London. This westerly route offered different scenery than our northbound journey - we traded the dramatic A1(M) corridor for views of the Irish Sea coast and the industrial northwest before heading southeast toward the capital.

The drive covered approximately 400 miles and took us 7.5 hours including rest stops, arriving at our destination - the Hilton Garden Inn Hatton Cross near Heathrow Airport - at 3:30 PM. The westerly route through Blackpool added some distance but provided variety to the journey. After over 700 miles of left-side driving throughout the trip, navigating British motorways and A-roads had become second nature.

The Edinburgh-London Return Drive: Starting at 8:00 AM, our route via Carlisle and Blackpool took approximately 7.5 hours with rest stops, arriving at 3:30 PM. The BMW 320 Station Wagon from SIXT performed excellently throughout the 700+ mile trip. By this point in the journey, roundabouts, narrow lanes, and motorway driving on the left felt completely natural.

The Journey South

The drive through Carlisle took us across the historic Scottish-English border region - territory that has seen centuries of conflict and now serves as a peaceful gateway between the two nations. From Carlisle, we continued southwest toward Blackpool, passing through Lancashire's varied landscapes.

After Blackpool, we turned southeast, bypassing Birmingham and its surrounding urban sprawl as we made our way through the Midlands toward Greater London. The motorways were efficient, and the BMW handled beautifully throughout the long drive. By mid-afternoon, we entered Greater London's orbital M25 motorway system, heading toward Heathrow Airport.

We checked into the Hilton Garden Inn Hatton Cross, conveniently located near Heathrow Airport for our morning departure. After checking in and settling into our room, it was time to return the rental car.

Returning the Rental Car

The SIXT rental car return was located at Heathrow Terminal 5. We drove the short distance from the hotel to the terminal, following the well-marked rental car return signs. The BMW 320 Station Wagon had been the perfect vehicle for our UK adventure - spacious enough for luggage, comfortable for long drives, and efficient on fuel. After over 700 miles through England and Scotland, we returned it without incident.

The SIXT return process at Terminal 5 was quick and efficient. We dropped off the car, collected our belongings, and prepared for our evening in central London. What had felt intimidating on Day 2 - driving on the left, navigating roundabouts, parking on narrow streets - now felt accomplished. We'd successfully completed a 700+ mile road trip through two countries, all from the right-hand seat.

Elizabeth Line to Central London

After dropping off the rental car at Terminal 5, we needed to get into central London for dinner. Our original plan was to use the Piccadilly Line, but we discovered it was closed for engineering works. Fortunately, London's newest addition - the Elizabeth Line - provided an excellent alternative.

We boarded the Elizabeth Line at Terminal 5 station, which offers a direct, high-speed connection into central London. The trains are modern, spacious, and comfortable - a significant upgrade from older Tube lines. The journey to Bond Street station took about 30 minutes, whisking us efficiently from Heathrow into the heart of the West End. The Elizabeth Line, opened in 2022, has transformed connections between Heathrow and central London with its fast, frequent service.

Elizabeth Line Travel Tip: When the Piccadilly Line is closed (common for weekend engineering works), the Elizabeth Line provides excellent Heathrow access. Terminal 5 to Bond Street takes ~30 minutes. Simply tap contactless payment or use an Oyster card. The modern trains are significantly more comfortable than older Tube stock.

Walking Through Mayfair

Exiting at Bond Street station, we found ourselves in the heart of Mayfair, one of London's most exclusive shopping districts. Even in January's early evening darkness, Mayfair exuded sophistication - elegant Georgian townhouses, luxury boutiques with carefully curated window displays, and the quiet confidence of one of the world's most expensive neighborhoods.

We walked through Mayfair's streets, past the flagship stores on Bond Street and through the quieter residential squares. The area represents peak London - heritage, wealth, and tradition combined with contemporary luxury. Unlike our jet-lagged rush through Westminster on Day 1, this evening stroll was relaxed and reflective. After eight days exploring the UK, we navigated London's streets with the confidence of experience rather than the uncertainty of newcomers.

Dinner at Jamavar

Our final dinner in London was at Jamavar London, a Michelin-starred Indian restaurant located in Mayfair. It was the perfect bookend to our UK journey - our first dinner had been at the Michelin 2-star Gymkhana, and our last would be at another acclaimed London Indian restaurant, this time showcasing the refined regional cuisines of India.

Jamavar, which holds a Michelin star, specializes in Indian royal court cuisine and regional specialties from across the subcontinent. The restaurant's elegant Mayfair setting provides a sophisticated backdrop for their meticulously prepared dishes. The name "Jamavar" refers to the intricate hand-woven shawls of Kashmir, and the restaurant's approach mirrors this craftsmanship - detailed, refined, and artful.

The menu at Jamavar showcases regional Indian cuisine with a focus on rare recipes and traditional cooking techniques executed at the highest level. From delicate kebabs to rich curries and expertly prepared breads, each dish demonstrated the depth and complexity of Indian culinary traditions. The presentation was elegant, the service attentive, and the flavors authentic yet refined.

As we enjoyed our final meal in the UK, we reflected on the culinary journey of the past nine days: Gymkhana's theatrical Indian cuisine in London, Dishoom in Manchester, Glasgow, and Edinburgh (three visits total), Sushisamba's fusion dining with Edinburgh views, Mouglee's modern Indian in Edinburgh, and now Jamavar's refined regional specialties. The trip had been bookended by two of London's finest Indian restaurants, showcasing the city's position as a global dining capital.

Return to Hilton Garden Inn

After dinner, we made our way back to the hotel using London's excellent public transport. We took the Elizabeth Line from Bond Street to Hayes & Harlington station, then connected to the 90 bus for the final leg to the Hilton Garden Inn Hatton Cross. The multi-modal journey demonstrated London's extensive transport network - even late in the evening, getting from central London to Heathrow was straightforward and efficient.

The bus 90 route runs frequently between Hayes & Harlington station and Heathrow's terminals, passing right by our hotel. The combination of Elizabeth Line and bus proved just as convenient as the closed Piccadilly Line would have been, and gave us one last opportunity to experience London's diverse transport options.

Back at our hotel near Heathrow, we packed our bags for the next morning's departure. The souvenirs were modest - Scottish shortbread, a few photos captured across 104 images that would help us relive the journey. But the real treasures were less tangible: the summit views from Arthur's Seat, the dramatic waterfall at Grey Mare's Tail, the stone circles of Stonehenge at dawn, Edinburgh's volcanic landscape, London's cosmopolitan energy, and the comfortable rhythm of a week well-traveled.

Departure

January 18th brought our departure back to Houston. The morning routine of international travel - early wake-up, final checks for forgotten items, the short journey to Heathrow Terminal. The airport, massive and efficient, handled our check-in with the smooth competence that makes Heathrow one of the world's busiest international hubs.

As our flight lifted off from London Heathrow, we watched the city spread below us - the Thames snaking through the urban landscape, the green spaces of Richmond Park and Hyde Park creating breathing room among the buildings, the distant outline of the Thames Estuary leading to the North Sea. Then England's coastline slipped away, and we were over water, heading west across the Atlantic toward home.

Nine days, five destinations, two countries, over 700 miles driven, five major hikes completed, and countless memories created. The United Kingdom had revealed itself in winter's honesty - not the warm sunny version promoted in tourism campaigns, but the authentic experience of crisp air, dramatic skies, cozy pubs, early sunsets, and landscapes that shift between bleak and beautiful by the hour.

Days 8-9 Activity: 2,645 steps covering 1.2 miles - mostly a driving day from Edinburgh to London (7.5 hours), with evening walking through Mayfair to Jamavar dinner and return via Elizabeth Line. Our lowest step count of the trip, giving our legs a well-deserved rest after nearly 91,000 steps over the previous seven days!

A Note on Photos: Unlike our other days, we didn't capture photos during Days 8-9. The focus was on the journey itself - the long drive south, the satisfaction of returning the rental car after a successful road trip, and savoring our final evening in London without the pressure of documentation. Sometimes the best travel moments are simply experienced rather than photographed. The memories of Mayfair's illuminated streets, Jamavar's refined cuisine, and the anticipation of returning home remain vivid even without visual records.
